AlphaDesk · Scoring Methodology

How composite scores are computed, what each metric measures, and why.
Current methodology version: 2026.06.2

1 · Overview

AlphaDesk rates every stock in its scan universe on a 0-100 composite score. The composite is a weighted average of five pillars, each of which is itself a weighted average of three to four metrics.

The five pillars represent five distinct lenses on the same business: fundamentals (is it healthy and fairly priced?), momentum (which way is the chart drifting?), sentiment (what does the rest of the market think?), filings tone (what is management writing differently this year?), and insider activity (are the people closest to the business buying or selling?).

Each pillar carries a weight that depends on the profile you select. A value-investor profile leans hard on fundamentals; a momentum profile flips that around. Same data, different question, different answer.

Sanity check: if a metric returns no data for a ticker, it does not default to zero. It is excluded from the pillar average, so the pillar is computed from whatever is available. The composite remains on a 0-100 scale.

2 · The Five Pillars

Fundamentals

Is the underlying business healthy and reasonably valued? Combines profitability, leverage, and valuation metrics.

Fundamentals separate companies that make real money from ones that survive on storytelling. Even if everything else looks great, weak fundamentals make a stock fragile under any market stress.

Momentum

Where is the stock in its recent trading range, and which way is price drifting?

Markets are not perfectly efficient — winners tend to keep winning for stretches, and beaten-down stocks can keep falling. Momentum captures this drift without becoming a pure trend-chase.

Sentiment

What does the rest of the market think? Aggregates analyst targets, short interest, and AI-summarized news sentiment.

Markets are a voting machine in the short term. If everyone hates a name, the price reflects that — and a fundamentals-only view will miss the catalyst when sentiment flips. Inversely, crowded longs can disappoint when news turns.

Filings Tone

How is management writing about the business? Compares year-over-year language in the 10-K Risk Factors and MD&A sections.

Management's word choice is signal. When Risk Factors get heavier year-over-year, or hedging phrases ("subject to", "no assurance") multiply, the people running the company are quietly telling you something the press release didn't.

Insider Activity

Open-market buys vs sales by company insiders over the last 90 days, sourced from SEC Form 4 filings.

Officers and directors have a hundred reasons to sell — taxes, diversification, kids in college. They have exactly one reason to buy: they think the stock goes up. Net buying — especially clustered and at meaningful dollar size — is one of the cleanest signals available because it's the people closest to the actual numbers.

3 · The Profiles

The profile selector reweights the pillars to match how you're thinking about the position. Same composite scale (0-100), different priorities.

4 · Metrics, Pillar by Pillar

Fundamentals metrics

P/E ratio lower better

Price divided by trailing twelve-month earnings.

How much you pay for each dollar of profit. A P/E of 15 means you wait 15 years (at today's earnings) to recoup. Lower-is-better with the caveat that growth companies command higher multiples for a reason.

FCF yield higher better

Free cash flow per share divided by share price.

The gold standard of valuation because — unlike earnings or revenue — it is almost impossible to fake. Currently null in our pipeline; planned upgrade.

ROIC (proxy: ROE) higher better

Return on equity — net income divided by shareholder equity.

How efficiently the company turns its capital into profit. The proxy (ROE) is imperfect — true ROIC would adjust for debt and operating leases — but the direction is reliable.

Gross margin higher better

Gross profit divided by revenue.

The most pricing-power-y metric in fundamental analysis. A 65% gross margin company has structural advantages — brand, network effects, patents — that a 20% gross margin company doesn't.

Debt / equity lower better

Total debt divided by shareholder equity.

Leverage. A little debt is fine — it's cheap capital. Too much and the company can't survive a downturn. Above 2.0 means more debt than equity, which historically correlates with worse drawdowns.

Momentum metrics

52-week position higher better

Where the current price sits in the 52-week high-to-low range.

Stocks near 52-week highs tend to keep working. Stocks near 52-week lows aren't automatically bargains — they may have broken below technical support for fundamental reasons.

Moving average trend higher better

Whether price > 50-day MA > 200-day MA (uptrend), or reversed.

The classic "price above all moving averages" setup that institutional rotation strategies use. Captures medium-term directional bias without overreacting to single-day noise.

Recent price change higher better

Percent change over the latest available window.

Short-term complement to the longer-window indicators. When all three momentum signals agree, the trend is durable; when they disagree, you may be catching a turning point.

Sentiment metrics

Analyst upside higher better

Percent gap between average Wall Street price target and the current price.

Sell-side analysts are wrong as often as they're right, but consensus target is a useful anchor. Large positive upside = analysts think the market is mispricing low. Large negative upside = even the cheerleaders think it's expensive.

Short interest lower better

Shares sold short as a percentage of float.

Smart money's bearish bet. Above 5% means meaningful skepticism; above 15% can either be a coiled spring (short squeeze) or a real warning (shorts often catch problems first).

Analyst consensus higher better

Buy/hold/sell consensus, AI-aggregated via Perplexity.

A strong-buy consensus reflects updated information from many analysts. Treat it as a sanity check, not a decision rule.

News sentiment higher better

Bullish/neutral/bearish read on the last 90 days of news.

Captures the tone of recent media coverage. A name with strong fundamentals but uniformly negative recent press deserves more scrutiny than the screener alone would suggest.

Filings tone metrics

10-K language drift lower better

TF-IDF cosine drift between this year and last year for Risk Factors + MD&A. 0 = identical, 100 = totally different.

Companies recycle most of their 10-K language verbatim. When they don't — when this year's Risk Factors are written from scratch — something material changed in their assessment of the business.

Hedging language lower better

YoY change in the frequency of hedging phrases per 1,000 words.

When management starts hedging more in writing, they're often preparing the disclosure ground for bad news. A rising hedging frequency is a quiet warning that often precedes guidance cuts or restructurings.

Insider activity metrics

Insider buy/sell ratio higher better

Open-market buys vs sales over the last 90 days, blended 60/40 between transaction count and dollar value.

The blend prevents one giant CEO sale from drowning out a chorus of smaller director buys, and vice versa.

Insider ownership higher better

Percent of shares outstanding held by insiders.

Skin in the game. Currently null in our pipeline; requires FMP institutional ownership tier.

Institutional ownership higher better

Percent of shares outstanding held by institutions.

Smart money concentration. Currently null in our pipeline.

5 · The Math

For each metric, the raw value is normalized to a 0-100 score using one of two methods:

  • Peer-percentile (live screener): the metric is ranked against every other ticker in the current scan. Top quintile → 80+, median → ~50, bottom quintile → below 20.
  • Absolute thresholds (historical backtest only): fixed cutoffs. e.g. PE < 12 → 95, PE 12-18 → 80, etc. Used in backtest because we don't have a historical peer universe to rank against.

The pillar score is the weighted average of its metric scores (weights renormalized over whichever metrics have data). The composite is the weighted average of pillar scores using the profile's pillar weights.

6 · Caveats and Limitations

  • FCF yield, insider ownership, and institutional ownership are not currently wired into the data pipeline. They will roll in as we add data sources. Until then they're flagged as n/a in the explainer.
  • Sentiment metrics (Perplexity-backed) only populate after you research a ticker (or the nightly cron does it for watchlist names). Newly screened tickers may show sentiment ≈ 50 until the cache catches up.
  • Filings tone requires two consecutive 10-Ks. Companies that recently IPO'd or restructured may show a baseline 60 until they have YoY history.
  • The historical backtest only reconstructs fundamentals + momentum + analyst upside. Filings, insider, and Perplexity sentiment cannot be reconstructed historically, so it tests about 60% of the methodology by weight.
  • Peer-percentile means a stock's score is relative to the current scan. The same stock will score differently if you change universeSize or sector filter. That's a feature, not a bug — but worth knowing.

7 · A Note on Using These Scores

Composite scores are a starting point, not a verdict. The whole point of the research panels attached to each Watchlist row — 10-K filing tone, Perplexity research card, insider transactions, historical backtest — is to interrogate why the score is what it is before you act on it.

A high composite with thin sentiment data is not the same as a high composite with triangulating evidence from all five pillars. The score is the headline. The research panels are the article.
